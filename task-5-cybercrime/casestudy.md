**Case Study: Ritu Kohli Cyberstalking Case**

Cyberbullying involves the repeated use of digital platforms—such as social media, texting apps, or online games—to harass, threaten, intimidate, or humiliate someone. Unlike traditional bullying, it follows victims anywhere via the internet, often anonymously. Legally, it's a crime in many places when it escalates to threats, defamation, or stalking, violating laws like the U.S. Children's Online Privacy Protection Act or India's IT Act Section 66A (struck down but similar provisions apply).
How It Typically Happens 
1.	Initiation: A bully identifies a vulnerability, like a teen posting about insecurities on Instagram.
2.	Escalation: They create fake accounts or rally friends to send mocking messages, memes, or altered photos.
3.	Amplification: Content spreads via shares, group chats, or viral posts, doxxing personal info like addresses.
4.	Intensification: Threats intensify (e.g., "We'll find you"), with 24/7 notifications overwhelming the victim.
5.	Sustainment: Bullies delete evidence or switch platforms to evade blocks.
<br>
Who Is Usually Targeted: - <br>
Primarily adolescents (ages 12-17), especially girls, LGBTQ+ youth, or those perceived as "different" due to appearance, race, or socioeconomic status. A 2023 Pew Research study found 59% of U.S. teens experienced online harassment.

<br>In India, one of the earliest and most well-known examples is the Ritu Kohli case, often described as the country’s first reported cyberstalking incident. In this case, a man named Manish Kathuria allegedly used Ritu Kohli’s identity in online chat rooms, posted obscene messages in her name, and shared her phone number with strangers.

<br>The crime typically happens in steps. First, the offender identifies the victim and collects personal information, such as photos, phone numbers, or social media accounts. Next, they create fake profiles or impersonate the victim online. Then they spread false or abusive content, invite others to contact the victim, and encourage harassment through calls, messages, or public humiliation. In Kohli’s case, this led to repeated obscene phone calls from unknown people and severe distress.

<br>Victims are usually teenagers, women, public figures, and anyone seen as vulnerable or easy to target. The effects can be serious: anxiety, fear, reputational damage, social withdrawal, and in extreme cases, depression or self-harm. Kohli reported the matter to the police, and the case became important because it showed how online abuse can cause real-world harm and how law enforcement must adapt to digital crimes
